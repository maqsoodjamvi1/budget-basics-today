For years, I tried to stay on top of my finances using whatever budget app looked the sleekest on the App Store. I’d set up my accounts, watch the colorful graphs for a week, and then realize I couldn’t actually do anything with the data. If I wanted to see my spending trends over three years or calculate a specific sub-category for a side hustle, the app’s built-in reports hit a dead end.

I eventually stopped relying on pre-packaged dashboards. Now, I use simple budgeting apps primarily as data-gathering tools, exporting everything into Excel or Google Sheets at the end of every month. This allows me to build custom pivot tables that actually reflect how I spend money, rather than how a developer thinks I should spend it.

If you are a spreadsheet power-user or just someone who wants to own your financial history, here is how to pick an app that acts as a bridge, not a walled garden.

Why Excel Still Beats the Best Budget Apps

Most apps thrive on “set it and forget it” automation, but they often struggle with nuance. For example, I have a specific “Home Improvement” category that I track against a five-year renovation plan. Most apps will bundle these expenses into a generic “House” or “Shopping” category, making it impossible to see my long-term progress.

By exporting to Excel, I can:

  • Create multi-year trends: Apps usually truncate data after 12 months. My spreadsheet tracks my net savings rate back to 2018.
  • Build custom tax reports: I can tag specific transactions as “deductible” throughout the year and run a filter in April to see the exact total without hunting through months of statements.
  • Control the format: I don’t want to see a pie chart of my coffee spending every time I log in. I want a table that shows my debt-to-income ratio, which I can tweak as my income fluctuates.

Choosing the Right Tool for Exporting

You don’t need an expensive financial suite to get your data out. In fact, the simpler the app, the cleaner the export usually is. When evaluating an app, look for two specific features: a “CSV Export” button and the ability to define a date range.

Tiller

Tiller is the gold standard if you want to skip the manual export step. It automatically pulls your bank transactions directly into a Google Sheet or Excel workbook. It acts as the “plumbing” between your bank and your spreadsheet. If you find the manual process of downloading files tedious, this is the most practical solution.

Lunch Money

This is a minimalist app that feels like a clean spreadsheet interface. It is incredibly developer-friendly. It allows you to export your entire ledger with one click. What I like about their CSV output is the clean formatting—the headers match standard accounting practices, meaning I rarely have to “clean” the data before pasting it into my master file.

Empower (formerly Personal Capital)

While many people use Empower for net-worth tracking, it is surprisingly good for high-level data exports. You can navigate to the “Transactions” tab, set a date range, and download the data as a CSV. It’s best for people who want to track large-scale investment performance alongside day-to-day spending.

How to Set Up Your Export Workflow

Don’t wait until the end of the year to export your data. If you wait 365 days, you will have to categorize hundreds of transactions at once.

Here is the rhythm that keeps me sane:

  1. The “Sunday Reset”: Every Sunday, I open my banking app and my budget app. I categorize any “uncategorized” transactions.
  2. The Monthly Pivot: On the first of every month, I log into my app’s web portal. I select the “Export to CSV” function for the previous month.
  3. The Master Copy: I paste that data into my Master Excel Workbook. I have a specific tab for “Raw Data” where I drop the exports, and a separate tab for “Reporting” that uses formulas to pull from that raw data.

What to Look for in the CSV File

Not all exports are created equal. When you test an app, perform a test export and open it in Excel. Make sure the file includes these columns:

  • Date: Essential for chronological sorting.
  • Merchant/Description: You need this to verify what the purchase actually was.
  • Category: If the app exports its own pre-assigned category, you can use a VLOOKUP in Excel to automatically map that to your own custom categories.
  • Amount: Make sure the app exports debits as negative numbers and credits as positive ones. If it exports everything as a positive number, you will have to write a custom formula to calculate your actual monthly balance.

Preparing for Next Steps

Taking control of your data means you are no longer at the mercy of an app’s interface updates or subscription price hikes. You are building a permanent record of your financial life that you can take with you regardless of which software you use.

Now that you have a plan for capturing your data, the next step is to choose the software that fits your transaction volume and reporting needs. Evaluate the options above based on your preferred level of automation, and consider how your current spreadsheet habits might evolve once you have a reliable stream of data to work with.
